## Module 1.5A – Utility Bills & Net Metering

Slide 1

Welcome to Module 1.5A — Utility Bills and Net Metering. Before you can explain why solar is a better financial choice, you need to understand exactly how a homeowner is currently being billed — and how solar changes that equation. The utility bill is the "before" picture. If you can't read it fluently, you can't credibly explain the "after." This module is your foundation. By the end, you will be able to pick up any utility bill, identify the key numbers in under two minutes, and explain net metering in language any homeowner can understand on the first try.

---

Slide 2

Every utility bill has four key components you need to locate immediately. First, total kilowatt-hour usage — this tells you how much power the home actually consumed that month. Second, the rate plan — is this a tiered rate, a flat rate, or a time-of-use structure? Third, the total amount due — broken into what they pay for energy delivery and what they pay for energy supply. And fourth, the twelve-month usage history — the most important row on the entire bill. Never ask the homeowner to interpret their own bill. You glance at it, find these four things immediately, and narrate what you see. That moment of fluency builds credibility before you've shown a single solar number.

---

Slide 3

There are two primary rate structures you will encounter. The first is tiered pricing — the more electricity a homeowner uses, the more expensive each additional kilowatt-hour becomes. The first block of usage is cheap. Every block after that costs more. This is actually great news for solar, because solar eliminates the most expensive tiers first. The second structure is time-of-use pricing — rates change depending on the time of day. Morning and late-night power is cheap. Afternoon and evening power is expensive. Under time-of-use, a solar system that produces during peak hours delivers maximum savings, and battery storage makes those savings even greater.

---

Slide 4

Net Energy Metering — NEM — is the engine that makes grid-tied solar financially viable. Here is the simplest way to explain it. Think of the grid like a bank account. During the day, your solar panels produce more electricity than your home uses. The excess gets sent to the grid — that's a deposit. At night, when your panels aren't producing, you pull power back from the grid — that's a withdrawal. At the end of each month, the utility settles the balance. If you deposited more than you withdrew, you carry a credit forward. If you withdrew more, you pay for the difference at retail rates. Solar paired with net metering essentially allows homeowners to use the grid as a free battery.

---

Slide 5

Not all net metering policies are equal — and this matters in the field. In states with one-to-one net metering, every kilowatt-hour a homeowner exports earns them a full retail credit — dollar for dollar. In states that have moved to partial credit metering — like California's NEM 3.0 — the export credit is much lower, sometimes just three to five cents per kilowatt-hour, compared to the twenty-five to forty cents they pay at retail. In those markets, battery storage becomes significantly more important because it allows the homeowner to use their own solar production instead of exporting it at a loss. Always know which policy applies in your market before you build a proposal.

---

Slide 6

The true-up bill is the annual settlement between the homeowner and the utility. Over the course of the year, solar credits accumulate when the system overproduces and the homeowner runs a surplus. At the end of the annual cycle, the utility reconciles the full year of credits and charges. If the homeowner overproduced, they may receive a small check — though typically at wholesale rates, not retail. If they underproduced, they owe the difference. This is why we size systems at one hundred five to one hundred ten percent of annual usage — to build in a cushion for degradation and seasonal variation. And one more important expectation to set: even with solar, most homeowners still have a small monthly connection fee — typically ten to twenty dollars. That is not the energy charge. That is the base cost of staying connected to the grid, and solar cannot eliminate it.

## Module 1.5B – Solar Financial Structures

Slide 1

Welcome to Module 1.5B — Solar Financial Structures. Solar is not a one-size-fits-all financial product. Your job is not to push one financing option. Your job is to match the right financial vehicle to the right homeowner based on their specific situation and goals. By the end of this module, you will be able to explain cash purchases, solar loans, leases, and power purchase agreements — clearly and confidently — and identify which option serves which homeowner best. Getting this wrong costs you deals. Getting it right builds trust that closes.

---

Slide 2

The core concept behind most solar financing is what we call "swapping the bill." The homeowner is already paying their utility company every single month — that money is gone forever. Solar replaces that ongoing utility payment with a fixed monthly payment for a system the homeowner will eventually own. The key question is never "can you afford solar?" — it's "does your solar payment cost less than what you're already paying?" In most qualifying homes, the answer is yes from day one. That's the swap. A forever utility bill replaced by a structured payment for an asset.

---

Slide 3

Before we go further, there is a compliance boundary every rep must understand. The thirty percent Federal Investment Tax Credit is real, and it is significant — on a thirty-thousand-dollar system, that is a nine-thousand-dollar credit. But you are not a CPA. You cannot guarantee a homeowner will receive it, tell them how to claim it, or advise them on their specific tax situation. The language you use must always stay within these boundaries. Say: "Most homeowners in this situation qualify for the thirty percent federal credit — but I always recommend confirming with your accountant before you count on it." That language is honest, helpful, and compliant.

---

Slide 4

A cash purchase is the highest-ROI option for homeowners who have the liquidity. The homeowner pays for the system in full, owns it outright immediately, and receives the full federal tax credit. There are no loan payments, no interest charges, and no escalator clauses. Over twenty-five years, a cash buyer saves the most money in absolute terms. The downside is obvious — it requires significant upfront capital. This option is best for homeowners with strong savings who are focused on maximum long-term return and have a clear tax liability to apply the credit against.

---

Slide 5

The solar loan is the most common financing option for qualified homeowners. The homeowner borrows the purchase price, owns the system, and receives the federal tax credit. Monthly loan payments are typically lower than their current utility bill from day one — which makes the math easy to demonstrate. The eighteen-month same-as-cash strategy works like this: some lenders offer a higher payment for the first eighteen months, then the homeowner applies their federal tax credit as a principal reduction when they file taxes. That drops the payment significantly for the remaining life of the loan. Walk homeowners through this math clearly — it answers the "is this worth it" question before they ask it. A dealer fee is built into the loan to buy down the interest rate — be transparent about this. The financed price is higher than the cash price, and homeowners deserve to understand why.

---

Slide 6

Solar leases and power purchase agreements — known as PPAs — are both third-party ownership structures. The solar company owns the system. The homeowner gets the electricity it produces. With a lease, the homeowner pays a fixed monthly amount regardless of how much the system produces. With a PPA, they pay per kilowatt-hour generated. Both options require zero down and zero maintenance responsibility. The tradeoff: the solar company keeps the federal tax credit, and both structures include an annual escalator clause that increases payments by two to three percent per year. This must always be disclosed. These options are best for homeowners with lower credit scores, those with no meaningful federal tax liability, or those who simply want the lowest possible monthly rate without the responsibilities of ownership.

## Module 1.7A – Basic Site Engineering Awareness

Slide 1

Welcome to Module 1.7A — Basic Site Engineering Awareness. One of the most common sources of post-sale friction is a site condition discovered at the professional survey that the rep should have identified during the sales visit. This module teaches you how to see what matters — not how to be an electrician or a structural engineer, but how to flag the right things at the right time. The principle is simple: see more, promise less, close better. When you catch a potential issue early and disclose it honestly, you protect the deal, build trust, and avoid surprises that kill customers' confidence in the process.

---

Slide 2

Start with the roof. The single most important factor is orientation — the direction the roof faces. In the Northern Hemisphere, a south-facing roof receives the most direct sunlight throughout the day and produces the most energy. East and west-facing roofs are still viable but produce less. North-facing roofs are generally not used for solar in most U.S. markets. Beyond orientation, assess the pitch — the steepness of the roof. A very flat or very steep roof may require special mounting hardware and can affect installation cost. And finally, look for shading. Trees, chimneys, neighboring structures, or HVAC units that cast shadow across the roof face during peak sun hours are your number one production variable. Shade that covers more than forty percent of usable roof area is a serious flag.

---

Slide 3

The electrical panel is the second critical observation. The number stamped on the main breaker tells you the service size. A two-hundred-amp panel is ideal for most residential solar installations. A one-hundred-amp panel may require what is called a Main Panel Upgrade — an additional cost that typically runs between two thousand and five thousand dollars depending on the market. This must be disclosed before the proposal is built, never after. Also check whether the panel has available breaker space. Even a two-hundred-amp panel with every slot occupied may require a subpanel or a line-side tap to accommodate the solar connection. Look for the main breaker number, count the open slots, and flag what you see.

---

Slide 4

The flag-versus-guess boundary is the most important professional discipline in this module. When you notice something — a small panel, a shaded roof, a roof that needs attention — your job is to flag it honestly, not diagnose it or quote a cost. Here is the language that keeps you in the right lane: "I noticed your panel is a hundred-amp service. That may require an upgrade before the install — our site survey team will confirm and give you an exact number." You have done two things. You have set an honest expectation. And you have positioned the survey team as the technical authority. What you should never do is guess at the cost, promise it won't be needed, or say "it'll probably be fine" when you're not certain.

---

Slide 5

Before you leave every property, run a quick mental checklist. Roof orientation — is the primary usable roof south or west facing? Shading — are there trees, structures, or obstructions that cast meaningful shade on the usable surface? Roof condition — are there visible signs of aging, missing shingles, moss, or sagging that suggest a re-roof conversation is needed first? Panel amperage — is this a one-hundred or two-hundred-amp service? Panel space — are there open breaker slots? This five-point check takes under two minutes. It protects your deal from post-survey surprises, builds your reputation as a thorough consultant, and makes your submissions cleaner and faster to approve.

## Module 3.7A – Technical Discovery Questions

Slide 1

Welcome to Module 3.7A — Technical Discovery Questions. Discovery is not only about uncovering financial motivation. It's also about surfacing the technical conditions that can derail a deal at site survey — before you spend forty-five minutes designing a system and building a proposal. This module gives you the specific questions that uncover roof history, structural concerns, electrical load, and future plans — framed as expert due diligence, not interrogation. A rep who asks these questions well is a rep who submits clean deals, avoids change orders, and builds a reputation with the operations team for quality.

---

Slide 2

The attic question is one of the most valuable and underused discovery tools in solar sales. Here is how to deliver it: "Have you ever had any roof leaks, or has anyone been in your attic recently to check the structure?" Why does this matter? Roof leak history and weak rafter structure are two of the most common reasons site surveys flag a home for additional engineering review or structural reinforcement. If you find this out now, you can set the expectation or disqualify before the site survey — instead of losing the deal after the survey team comes back with bad news. Frame it professionally: "I ask because the installation team needs to attach directly to your roof structure, and any existing damage would come up in the survey. Better we know now."

---

Slide 3

The utility bill reflects what the homeowner consumes today — but not what they'll consume in the future. The electrical load discovery question opens that door: "Are you planning on getting an electric vehicle in the next few years? Or do you have a pool, hot tub, or anything else that might significantly add to your electric usage?" If they say yes to any of these, you've just opened a larger system conversation. More panels, better production, bigger savings — and a higher commission on a system that actually serves their real life, not just their current bill. Position this as expertise: "I always ask because a lot of homeowners get solar sized for today and then realize the system isn't quite big enough after they get an electric vehicle. I'd rather design it right the first time."

---

Slide 4

The SURPRISE prevention script is how you position all technical discovery questions as professional due diligence rather than a sales script. Before you ask, set the frame: "As part of my standard process before I build a proposal, I always run through a few technical questions — just so nothing comes as a surprise during the site survey. Mind if I take two minutes?" That one sentence transforms what follows. You're not grilling them with questions. You're doing what a professional consultant does — making sure the numbers you build are based on reality, not assumptions. Homeowners almost always say yes to this framing. And every honest answer they give you makes your proposal cleaner, your survey smoother, and your install faster.

## Module 3.7B – Site Survey Prep for Reps

Slide 1

Welcome to Module 3.7B — Site Survey Prep for Reps. A clean deal is one that passes through the professional site survey without change orders, delays, or redesigns. The quality of your pre-survey documentation directly affects how fast the project moves — and how likely the homeowner is to stay sold during the waiting period. This module teaches you exactly what photos and information to gather before you leave every appointment. Reps who submit clean packages get faster installs, happier customers, and lower cancellation rates. Reps who submit sloppy packages create delays that frustrate homeowners and generate chargebacks.

---

Slide 2

There are five critical photos every rep should take before leaving the property — no exceptions. Photo one: the main electrical panel, door open, with the main breaker rating clearly visible and all breaker labels legible. Photo two: the utility meter face — digital display or dials — close enough to read the meter number. Photo three: the attic structure if accessible — showing the rafters and decking condition for the engineering team. Photo four: the home's surroundings — from the backyard or the street, capturing any trees, structures, or HVAC units that could cause shading on the roof. And photo five: the street-to-home view, showing how the utility service wire or underground service enters the property. Five photos. Every appointment. These take under three minutes and they prevent weeks of back-and-forth with the engineering team.

---

Slide 3

After you've taken the five photos, take a moment to note any attic access points and the proximity of the electrical panel to where the inverter will likely be mounted. The site surveyor needs to know whether the attic is accessible for structural assessment, and whether the panel is in a location that makes the solar wiring path straightforward or complex. If you observed a subpanel during the visit, photograph that too. The more context you give the engineering team before they arrive, the smoother and faster the survey goes — and the fewer surprises that generate change orders.

---

Slide 4

Before you leave every appointment, set the homeowner's expectation for what comes next. Here is the script: "The next step after today is a professional site survey. A trained technician will come out and do a full assessment of your roof and electrical system. This usually happens within seven to ten business days. They'll need about an hour, and they'll need access to your electrical panel and attic if there is one. You don't need to be home for the whole visit, but we do need access. Does that timeline work for you?" This three-sentence script does two things: it normalizes the next step so the homeowner isn't surprised by a stranger showing up at their house, and it confirms their participation — which keeps them engaged in the process and much less likely to cancel during the wait.

## Module 4.1A – Presentation Structure & Flow

Slide 1

Welcome to Module 4.1A — Presentation Structure and Flow. A solar presentation is not a data dump. It is a narrative journey — and like any journey, it needs a clear path from start to finish. When a presentation loses structure, homeowners get lost. When they get lost, they feel confused. And confused homeowners don't sign. This module teaches you the five-pillar presentation flow that keeps homeowners engaged, builds logical momentum, and leads naturally to a decision — without pressure, without rushing, and without skipping the steps that build trust.

---

Slide 2

Every solar presentation follows five pillars in sequence. Pillar one is the personal introduction and re-discovery — you reconnect with why the homeowner is open to this conversation, and you re-establish yourself as a consultant. Pillar two is the macro problem — utility rates, corporate monopoly, year-over-year inflation that the homeowner has no control over. Pillar three is the solar solution — how it works, the equipment, and the customized design for their specific home. Pillar four is the company story — why your company, why now, and why this is a relationship not just a transaction. And pillar five is the financial logic — monthly payment versus monthly bill, return on investment, and ownership. Every pillar builds on the one before it. Skip one and the foundation cracks.

---

Slide 3

Transitions are the invisible scaffolding of a great presentation. Without them, you're jumping from topic to topic and the homeowner has to work to follow you. With them, each section flows naturally into the next. A transition of power is a specific phrase that closes one pillar and opens the next — while maintaining momentum. An example: "So now that you can see exactly what your roof is capable of producing — let me show you what that actually means for your monthly payment." That one sentence closes the design section and opens the financial section without a beat. Practice your transitions until they feel as natural as the content itself. The presentation lives or dies on the connective tissue between pillars.

---

Slide 4

Cognitive load management is about knowing how much information is too much. Most reps err in one direction: they over-explain. They go too deep into specs when the homeowner needs a headline. They show too many numbers when one clear comparison would close the deal. The rule is this — go deep when the homeowner signals they want depth, and stay high-level when they want clarity. An Owl personality wants the degradation curve. A Bull personality wants the monthly savings number. A Lion personality wants to know how their neighbors reacted when they saw the system. Read the room, and match your depth to your audience.

---

Slide 5

The assumptive pivot is the transition from your system design into the financing conversation. Most reps make this transition awkward by asking permission: "Would it be okay if we talked about the financial options?" That question puts the homeowner in a position to say no. The assumptive pivot removes that option. After you've confirmed the design looks good with a micro-close, move directly: "Okay — let me pull up the numbers and show you exactly what this looks like on a monthly basis." You're not asking. You're guiding. That shift from asking to guiding is what separates reps who close from reps who present and hope.

## Module 4.2A – Technical Specs for Non-Techies

Slide 1

Welcome to Module 4.2A — Technical Specs for Non-Techies. Homeowners do not buy technical specifications. They buy what those specifications do for them. No one has ever signed a solar contract because they were impressed by the phrase "n-type monocrystalline silicon." They signed because they understood what that panel does for their home, their bill, and their peace of mind over the next twenty-five years. This module teaches you how to translate every technical feature into a homeowner benefit — in plain language that lands on the first explanation, not the third.

---

Slide 2

Panel efficiency tells you how much electricity a panel produces per square foot of roof space. A higher-efficiency panel produces more power using less roof area. Here is how to explain it: "These panels are more efficient than the standard option — which means we can fit the right system on your roof even if you have limited usable space." You've communicated the benefit without mentioning a single percentage point. When a homeowner has a smaller or partially shaded roof, efficiency becomes a tangible advantage — and now they understand why. Always lead with what it does, not what it is.

---

Slide 3

The inverter is the piece of equipment that converts your solar panel's DC electricity into the AC electricity your home actually uses. There are two main types. String inverters convert the output of all panels together as a group. Microinverters convert each panel individually. Here is the analogy that makes this click for every homeowner: "Think of string inverters like old Christmas lights — if one bulb goes out, the whole string dims. Microinverters are like the newer LED lights — each bulb is independent. If one panel gets shade, the others keep performing at full power." That analogy takes a complex engineering concept and makes it immediately understandable — and it gives homeowners a reason to care about which type they're getting.

---

Slide 4

Every solar panel has a performance warranty that guarantees how much power it will still produce after twenty-five years. This is called the degradation rate — the small percentage of output the panel loses each year. Premium panels degrade at roughly half a percent per year, meaning after twenty-five years they're still producing around eighty-seven percent of their original output. Here is how to frame it: "This system comes with a twenty-five-year performance guarantee. Even a quarter century from now, these panels will still be producing at high efficiency. That's not a sales claim — it's a written warranty backed by the manufacturer." The warranty is your proof. Use it.

## Module 4.2B – Equipment Competitive Comparison

Slide 1

Welcome to Module 4.2B — Equipment Competitive Comparison. In a competitive solar market, homeowners get multiple quotes. And sometimes, a competitor's quote is cheaper. Your job is not to panic, not to match the price, and not to badmouth the competition. Your job is to help the homeowner make a truly informed comparison — one that goes beyond the upfront number to the twenty-five-year value they are actually purchasing. This module teaches you how to win against cheaper alternatives by shifting the conversation from price to value, using a consultative approach that positions you as the advisor in the room.

---

Slide 2

Not all solar panels are built to the same standard. The market has two broad tiers. Value-tier equipment typically has higher degradation rates, shorter warranties, and less proven field performance. Premium-tier equipment — brands like REC, Maxeon, and Q-Cells — carries longer performance warranties, lower degradation curves, and stronger manufacturer backing. The gap matters over time. A system that degrades two percent faster per year produces meaningfully less electricity over twenty-five years — which translates directly into less savings. The homeowner who chose the cheaper quote may find they've saved money upfront and lost money over the life of the system.

---

Slide 3

When homeowners mention competing brands, know the key differentiators. REC panels carry a strong product and performance warranty combination and are well regarded for efficiency in partial-shade environments. Maxeon — formerly SunPower — builds one of the most durable and efficient panels on the market, with one of the lowest degradation rates available. Q-Cells are a strong mid-tier option with solid European engineering heritage. Tesla panels are vertically integrated and come with the Tesla ecosystem, but are best suited to homeowners already invested in that platform. When a homeowner brings up a competitor, ask what brand they're quoting — and then have a specific, factual comparison ready. Specific beats generic every time.

---

Slide 4

When a homeowner tells you they received a quote five or eight thousand dollars less than yours, the consultative pivot sounds like this: "That's worth looking at carefully — can I walk you through a few comparison points together?" Then go through the checklist: What's the panel brand and efficiency rating? What's the degradation warranty — eighty or ninety percent at twenty-five years? Who is actually doing the installation — the company directly, or a subcontractor? What monitoring system is included? What's the cancellation and service policy if you have a warranty claim in year twelve? Often the homeowner has never been asked these questions before. When you walk them through the comparison honestly, you're not attacking the competitor — you're demonstrating your expertise. And expertise is what earns the close.

## Module 4.4A – NEM 3.0 & Battery Sales Logic

Slide 1

Welcome to Module 4.4A — NEM 3.0 and Battery Sales Logic. Net metering rules have changed significantly in major solar markets — and the rep who understands those changes builds credibility. The rep who ignores them gets burned by a homeowner who researched it online before the appointment. In California and other states that have adopted NEM 3.0, the financial equation for solar has shifted. This module teaches you exactly what changed, why it matters, and how to position battery storage as the solution — naturally, honestly, and without manufacturing urgency that doesn't exist.

---

Slide 2

Under the original NEM 2.0 structure, homeowners received credit for every kilowatt-hour they exported to the grid at close to full retail rates. A solar-only system could cover nearly all of a homeowner's annual energy costs through those credits. NEM 3.0 changed that. In California, export credits dropped by roughly seventy-five percent — from around thirty cents per kilowatt-hour down to three to five cents. That means a solar-only system that exports a large portion of its production during the day earns far less credit than it used to. The financial model has not disappeared — but it has changed. A rep who walks into a NEM 3.0 market still pitching NEM 2.0 math will lose credibility the moment the homeowner checks their numbers.

---

Slide 3

The solution to low export credits is battery storage. A battery allows the homeowner to store their excess solar production instead of exporting it to the grid at a loss. They use that stored power at night — when they would otherwise be pulling expensive retail-rate electricity from the grid. In a NEM 3.0 market, this is not a luxury add-on. It is often the move that makes the financial case for solar whole again. The pitch is straightforward: "Under the current net metering policy, exporting your excess power earns you very little credit. A battery keeps that power in your home — so instead of selling it cheap and buying it back expensive, you're using your own energy around the clock."

---

Slide 4

Not every homeowner in a NEM 3.0 market needs a battery — and honesty here is what separates a consultant from a salesperson. A homeowner with a very time-shifted load profile — one that uses most of their power in the evening and overnight — benefits most from battery storage. A homeowner who uses the majority of their power during the day, while the system is actively producing, has less export and may see a more modest benefit. Your job is to model both scenarios honestly and show the homeowner what their specific numbers look like with and without storage. When the data supports battery storage, the homeowner will see it. Let the numbers do the work.

## Module 5.5A – Advanced Closing Scripts

Slide 1

Welcome to Module 5.5A — Advanced Closing Scripts. The close is not a single question at the end of a presentation. It is a series of micro-commitments that build momentum throughout the conversation. By the time you reach the final decision, a homeowner who has been saying yes to small things is far more likely to say yes to the big one. This module gives you the linguistic frameworks to maintain that momentum — language that guides without pressuring, and closes without forcing. The goal is a homeowner who feels confident, clear, and in control — because that is the homeowner who stays sold.

---

Slide 2

The choice close is one of the most effective tools in the final ten minutes of an appointment. Instead of asking "do you want to move forward?" — which is a yes-or-no question that invites no — you give the homeowner a choice between two paths that both lead forward. "Based on what you've told me, I'd recommend the twenty-year loan — but some homeowners in your situation prefer the ten-year term for the faster payback. Which feels more aligned with how you like to handle financial decisions?" You're not asking if they want solar. You're asking which version of solar fits them better. That shift from "if" to "which" is one of the most valuable language adjustments you'll ever make.

---

Slide 3

Urgency must be real to be effective. Manufactured urgency — "this deal ends Friday" or "my manager only approved this price for today" — destroys trust the moment a homeowner senses it isn't true. Real urgency comes from external factors you can document. Federal tax credit step-downs. Upcoming utility rate increases that have been publicly announced. Seasonal installation backlogs that push timelines out. When you tie urgency to something verifiable, homeowners respond to it as information — not as pressure. The script sounds like this: "The federal credit is locked at thirty percent through 2032, but the install backlog in this area is currently running ten to twelve weeks. Homeowners who move forward in the next few weeks get ahead of the summer rush."

---

Slide 4

Buying signals are behavioral cues that tell you the homeowner is ready to decide — before they say so. Watch for these: leaning forward toward the screen, asking future-tense questions like "when would installation start," looking at their spouse with a nod, asking about financing options unprompted, or picking up your tablet to look more closely at the design. When you see three or more of these at once, slow down. Stop advancing through your presentation. Move directly to the close. The rep who keeps pitching after the homeowner has mentally decided is the rep who talks them back out of it. Spot the signal. Stop the pitch. Ask for the decision.

## Module 5.5B – Referral Science: Self-Sustaining Pipeline

Slide 1

Welcome to Module 5.5B — Referral Science. The most expensive lead you'll ever work is the one you buy or generate through cold prospecting. The most valuable lead you'll ever receive is the one a happy customer hands you. Referred leads close at two to three times the rate of cold leads because the trust has already been established before you say a single word. This module teaches you the science of building referrals systematically — not asking when you happen to remember, but building a repeatable process that generates pipeline from every single deal you close.

---

Slide 2

There are three magic windows for referral requests — specific moments in the client relationship when homeowners are at peak satisfaction and most likely to refer. The first is immediately after the contract is signed — the dopamine moment. "I'm really excited for you — this is going to make a real difference. If anyone you know has been complaining about their electric bill, I'd love an introduction. Can I use your name when I reach out?" The second window is install day, when the crew is on the roof and the project is visually real. The third is PTO — Permission to Operate day, when the system goes live for the first time. Ask at all three. Each one yields different referrals from different emotional states.

---

Slide 3

The trust-based referral ask is different from a transactional one. A transactional ask sounds like: "Do you know anyone who wants solar?" A trust-based ask sounds like this: "You made a really smart decision today. Not everyone is in the right situation for solar — but you were a clear fit. Are there two or three people in your life who might be in a similar situation? I won't pitch them — I'll just have an honest conversation the same way I had one with you." That framing does three things. It validates their decision. It positions you as selective and trustworthy. And it makes the referral feel like a favor to their friend — not a lead for a salesperson.

---

Slide 4

Referral incentives can work — but only when they're used correctly. A monetary incentive offered before the referral is asked can feel transactional and lower the quality of the leads. A referral bonus delivered as a surprise after the referral's deal closes feels like a genuine thank-you. If your company has a formal referral program, mention it — but lead with the relationship, not the reward. "I wanted to let you know we have a referral bonus program — but honestly, the reason I'm asking is because I think your neighbor would genuinely benefit from this the same way you did." Relationship first, incentive second. That's the order that generates quality referrals.

## Module 5.5C – Post-Sale Communication & Reviews

Slide 1

Welcome to Module 5.5C — Post-Sale Communication and Reviews. The contract signature is not the end of the sale. It is the beginning of the customer experience. Between the day a homeowner signs and the day their system goes live, sixty to ninety days can pass. That is sixty to ninety days where silence, confusion, or unmet expectations can turn a closed deal into a cancellation. This module teaches you how to manage that window proactively — keeping homeowners engaged, preventing buyer's remorse, and turning satisfied customers into five-star reviews that generate your next deal.

---

Slide 2

Buyer's remorse happens most often in the first seventy-two hours after a contract is signed. The homeowner's excitement fades, doubt creeps in, and if no one reaches out to reinforce the decision, that doubt grows into a cancellation call. The forty-eight-hour inoculation call is your prevention tool. Within forty-eight hours of the signature, call the homeowner. Keep it brief and warm: "Hey, I just wanted to check in and make sure you're feeling good about everything. Do you have any questions that came up since we talked?" This one call reduces cancellations dramatically because it shows you're still present, you care, and you're going to be a reliable partner through the process.

---

Slide 3

Throughout the installation process, there are several milestone moments where a proactive update from you keeps the homeowner engaged and confident. After the site survey: let them know it went well and what the next step is. When permits are submitted: tell them the timeline. When permits are approved: celebrate it with them — this is real progress. The week before installation: confirm the date and remind them what access the crew will need. After installation: acknowledge the milestone and explain that inspection is next. After inspection and PTO: this is the celebration message. System is live. Send the monitoring app link. Ask for the review. Every milestone message is a touchpoint that builds loyalty and reduces cancellation risk.

---

Slide 4

The best time to ask for a five-star review is when the homeowner's emotions are at their peak — and that peak happens before the system is even turned on. Research shows that homeowners are most enthusiastic immediately after signing and immediately after the install is complete, before the novelty wears off. Here is the ask: "Before I let you go — if your experience with me so far has been a good one, would you be willing to leave us a review? It takes about sixty seconds and it helps other homeowners in your neighborhood find a rep they can trust. Here's the direct link." Send it immediately while they're in that emotional high. A frictionless link, delivered at the right moment, converts to a review far more often than a follow-up request weeks later.

## Module 5.5D – Change Order Management

Slide 1

Welcome to Module 5.5D — Change Order Management. Engineering discoveries happen. A site surveyor finds that a roof can support fewer panels than planned. A panel upgrade requirement comes back from the electrical inspection. A structural concern changes the design. Change orders are a normal part of the solar installation process — but how you deliver that news determines whether the homeowner stays sold or cancels. This module teaches you how to communicate bad technical news in a way that preserves the relationship, maintains trust, and gives the homeowner a clear path forward.

---

Slide 2

The bad-news-good-news sandwich is your primary delivery structure for change orders. You never lead with the cost. You start by acknowledging the situation honestly, then explain what it means, then pivot to the solution. Here is an example: "So I heard back from our engineering team after the site survey — there's something I want to walk you through." That is the acknowledgment. Then: "They found that the south-facing roof face can support ten panels instead of twelve — which slightly reduces the system's annual output." That is the honest information. Then immediately: "The good news is the updated design still covers approximately ninety percent of your usage — and your savings projection only adjusts by about twelve dollars a month. Here's what the updated proposal looks like." That is the path forward. Bad news first. Context second. Solution third.

---

Slide 3

If the change order involves an additional cost — like a main panel upgrade or a structural reinforcement — the framing becomes even more important. Never present the cost without the context of why it matters. "The survey team flagged that your panel is a hundred-amp service. To safely connect the solar system, it needs to be upgraded to two hundred amps. That's an additional cost, but here's the important part — that upgrade is something your home likely needs regardless of solar. You're essentially getting the electrical update your home needs as part of the same project, rather than dealing with it separately down the road." You're reframing an additional cost as a legitimate home improvement. That framing is honest and it helps the homeowner see value instead of just a number.

---

Slide 4

Avoid what we call rep blame — the instinct to create distance between yourself and the bad news by implying the problem is someone else's fault. Phrases like "the engineering team should have caught this earlier" or "I'm not sure why the survey took so long" undermine the homeowner's confidence in the entire company. Instead, own it as a team: "This kind of discovery is exactly why the professional site survey exists — to make sure we build the right system the right way." You are positioning the process as working correctly, not failing. And when you deliver the change order with calm, prepared professionalism, you become the person the homeowner trusts to guide them through it — not the person they hold responsible for it.

## Module 6.1A – Referral System Architecture

Slide 1

Welcome to Module 6.1A — Referral System Architecture. The difference between a rep who gets one referral per ten deals and a rep who gets three to five is not personality — it's structure. Most reps treat referrals as a reaction. They ask when they happen to remember, or when the homeowner seems particularly happy. A systematic rep builds referral requests into every stage of the client relationship as a standard process — not an afterthought. This module gives you that architecture: three trigger moments, proven scripts, and a follow-through process that compounds over time.

---

Slide 2

There are three trigger moments where referral requests are most natural and most effective. The first is immediately after closing — the dopamine moment, when the homeowner is excited and confident in their decision. The second is install day, when the project is physically real and visible to the neighborhood. Neighbors notice a crew on the roof. They ask questions. Your homeowner becomes a brand ambassador. The third is PTO day — when the system goes live and the homeowner sees real-time production on their monitoring app for the first time. That moment of "it's actually working" creates peak enthusiasm. Ask at all three. Different homeowners will respond at different stages.

---

Slide 3

The referral introduction protocol is how you convert a name into a warm outreach. When a homeowner gives you a referral, always ask for permission to use their name: "Can I mention that you suggested I reach out?" Then reach out personally — not with a generic company template. "Hey Sarah — I was just helping your neighbor, the Johnsons on Maple Street — they mentioned you might be in a similar situation with your utility bill and suggested I give you a call. I'm not here to pitch anything — just to have the same honest conversation I had with them." That one message opens at a completely different level than any cold outreach. The trust is borrowed from the relationship that already exists.

---

Slide 4

Over time, a systematic referral process creates compounding pipeline. The rep who closes ten deals a month with a zero-referral rate has to cold-prospect for every single new lead. The rep who closes ten deals a month and generates two referrals per deal enters month two with twenty warm leads already in their pipeline — before they've knocked a single door. The math compounds: more referrals mean warmer leads, warmer leads close faster and at higher rates, higher close rates generate more customers, more customers generate more referrals. This is the flywheel that separates reps earning forty thousand dollars a year from reps earning forty thousand dollars a month. Build the system from deal one.

## Module 6.2A – Review Generation & Social Proof Systems

Slide 1

Welcome to Module 6.2A — Review Generation and Social Proof Systems. Ninety percent of homeowners check Google reviews before returning a solar rep's call. A profile with forty-seven five-star reviews and a profile with twelve reviews are not close competitors — they are in different conversations entirely. Reviews are not a marketing team problem. They are a rep's responsibility. Every satisfied homeowner who does not leave a review is a missed lead generation opportunity. This module teaches you when to ask, how to ask, and how to use social proof actively in the field to close deals you wouldn't have closed without it.

---

Slide 2

The best time to ask for a review is at an emotional high point in the customer journey — not weeks after the install when the novelty has faded. There are three high points. The first is immediately after the contract is signed. The second is after the install is complete. The third is after PTO, when the system goes live. At each moment, the homeowner is experiencing real satisfaction. The ask should be frictionless and personal: "If your experience with me so far has been a good one, would you be willing to take sixty seconds to leave us a review? I'll send you the direct link right now." Send the link immediately — in the room, before you leave the driveway. Delayed ask equals missed review.

---

Slide 3

Once you have reviews, use them actively in the field. Screenshot positive reviews — especially ones from homeowners in your territory — and keep them in your tablet presentation. When you're at a door in a neighborhood where you have an install, say: "Here's what John said after we installed his system two streets over." Testimonials are social proof that no product spec or savings calculation can replace. They answer the homeowner's deepest unspoken question: "Is this real? Did it work for someone like me?" A neighbor's name on a review answers that question in a way that nothing you say about yourself can. Build your review library intentionally and deploy it strategically.

---

Slide 4

Yard signs are passive social proof at scale. A yard sign on a completed install tells every car that drives down that street, every neighbor who walks their dog, and every homeowner who looks out their window — someone on this street made a decision. It creates social proof without you being present. Ask every homeowner for a thirty-day yard sign: "Would you mind if we left a sign in your yard for about a month? It helps your neighbors see that someone in the area went solar, and it helps us — kind of a win-win." Most homeowners say yes. A yard sign typically generates two to five additional lead conversations in the surrounding area. Combined with your Google reviews, this turns one install into a neighborhood momentum play that requires no additional cold prospecting.

## Module 6.3A – Orphan Owner Reactivation Playbook

Slide 1

Welcome to Module 6.3A — Orphan Owner Reactivation Playbook. An orphan owner is a homeowner who purchased solar through your company but whose original rep is no longer with the organization. They have a live solar system, they're paying their monthly bill or loan, and they have no active rep relationship. They receive monitoring alerts, billing questions, and occasionally have concerns — with no assigned point of contact. This is both a service failure and a significant revenue opportunity. This module teaches you how to reactivate those relationships professionally, build genuine value, and turn orphan owners into referral sources, upgrade customers, and loyal advocates.

---

Slide 2

Why do orphan owners matter? First, they already trust the company — they made a major purchase decision and went through the full installation process. Second, they have proven they are willing to invest in their home. Third, they can see their system's performance data every day — which means they have ongoing questions and engagement points that a good rep can step into. Fourth, their neighbors can see their system. Orphan owners sit in neighborhoods where your company already has visibility. The rep who reactivates an orphan owner relationship often gets access to an entire street of warm referrals from someone who has been living with a solar system and talking about it for years.

---

Slide 3

The reactivation outreach is not a sales call. It is a service call. The framing must be genuine: "Hi, this is [your name] with [company name]. I noticed you had a system installed with us a couple of years ago and I wanted to reach out personally — I'm the new rep covering your area, and I just wanted to make sure you're getting everything you need. How has the system been working for you?" Listen. Let them talk. If there are concerns, address them or escalate them to operations. If everything is great, affirm it: "That's great to hear — the monitoring data looks really solid on your end too." You are building relationship credit before you ask for anything.

---

Slide 4

After the relationship is re-established, there are three natural upsell conversations. Battery storage — especially relevant in NEM 3.0 markets or areas with frequent outages. Panel expansion — if their usage has increased due to an electric vehicle, a home addition, or a pool. And EV charging equipment — if they recently purchased or are considering an electric vehicle. In each case, lead with their data: "Looking at your monitoring history, I noticed your consumption has gone up about twenty percent over the past year. Have you added anything significant to the home?" That question opens the conversation naturally. And always: ask for referrals. "Have any of your neighbors asked you about the system? I'd love to help them — and you'd get the referral credit."

## Module 6.4A – KPI Mastery & Self-Coaching System

Slide 1

Welcome to Module 6.4A — KPI Mastery and Self-Coaching System. The reps who build long-term careers in solar are not always the most talented — they are the most self-aware. They know exactly where their deals are dying. They know their close rate, their show rate, their set rate — and they know which one to work on this week. A rep who cannot identify their own bottleneck is a rep who will repeat the same mistakes indefinitely and wonder why results don't change. This module gives you the six core KPIs you must track, and a weekly self-coaching framework that turns your own data into your most valuable coaching tool.

---

Slide 2

There are six core KPIs every solar rep must track weekly without exception. Doors knocked — the activity that starts everything. Conversations — how many homeowners actually engaged beyond "not interested." Appointments set — how many agreed to a specific time. Appointments shown — how many actually happened. Proposals presented — how many full presentations were delivered. And deals closed — how many contracts were signed. Each ratio between these metrics reveals a specific leak in your process. Low conversations per door means your opener needs work. Low show rate means your confirmation process is broken. Low close rate means your presentation or objection handling is the weak link. The numbers tell you exactly what to practice.

---

Slide 3

The weekly self-coaching session is a fifteen-minute Friday ritual that is worth more than most team trainings. Ask yourself five questions. Did I hit one hundred doors this week — and if not, what specifically prevented it? Did I set eight or more appointments — and if not, where did the conversation break down? Did I show up to every scheduled appointment — and if not, what happened to my confirmation process? Did I close at least one deal — and if not, where exactly did I lose them? And: do I have ten or more appointments scheduled for next week — and if not, I am behind. These five questions force honest self-assessment. They replace the emotional narrative of "it was a tough week" with specific, actionable data.

---

Slide 4

The income projection exercise is how you connect your KPIs to your financial goals — and make the relationship between activity and income concrete and motivating. Start with your income goal. Divide by your average commission per deal. That gives you the number of closed deals you need per month. Divide by your close rate to get the appointments you need. Divide by your show rate to get the appointments you need to set. Divide by your set rate to get the conversations you need. Divide by your conversation rate to get the doors you need to knock. Every number in that chain is knowable — and controllable. When you see that your income goal requires ninety-seven doors per week and you knocked sixty-two last week, the gap isn't motivational. It's mathematical. And math has a solution.
