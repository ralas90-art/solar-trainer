## 🗺️ THE SOLAR CONVERSATION FRAMEWORK

> **Introduce this on Day 2. Reference it every day. Make it muscle memory.**

Every solar conversation—whether at the door, on the phone, or inside a home—follows the same repeatable arc. This is your GPS. You may speed through some steps or linger longer on others depending on the homeowner, but you never skip a step.

| Step | Name | Purpose |
|------|------|---------|
| 1 | **Pattern Interrupt** | Break their autopilot "not interested" response |
| 2 | **Rapport** | Create safety and connection before selling anything |
| 3 | **Curiosity Question** | Get them talking about their energy situation |
| 4 | **Problem Awareness** | Help them see the pain they've been tolerating |
| 5 | **Utility Bill Discovery** | Quantify the problem with real numbers |
| 6 | **Qualification** | Confirm they're a fit (home, credit, decision-maker) |
| 7 | **Appointment / Next Step** | Secure commitment to continue the conversation |

This framework applies at the door (Steps 1–3 compressed into 60 seconds), on the phone, and in the full in-home consultation. Reps will practice each step in isolation before putting them together.

#### ⭐ Module 2.1 – Homeowner Psychology: Why People Resist Solar (35 min)

> **NEW MODULE — Critical foundation for everything that follows.**

This is one of the most important modules in the entire program. Reps who understand *why* homeowners resist are far more effective than reps who only know *what* to say. Before you can persuade, you must understand.

**Objectives:**
- Understand the psychological barriers homeowners bring to every solar conversation
- Recognize resistance as normal human behavior, not personal rejection
- Use this knowledge to build trust and reduce friction throughout every interaction

---

**2.1a – Why Homeowners Resist Change**

Most homeowners are not actively looking for a solar salesperson. When you show up—especially at their door—you are interrupting their routine. Their brain's default mode is *preserve the status quo*. Change feels risky. Inaction feels safe.

Key psychological drivers of resistance to change:

- **Status quo bias:** The current situation feels safer than an unknown future, even if the current situation is objectively worse.
- **Decision avoidance:** Making a decision creates anxiety. Saying "I'll think about it" relieves that anxiety—temporarily.
- **Risk aversion:** Homeowners fear being wrong more than they want to be right. A bad decision feels worse than missing a good opportunity.

**What this means for you:** Don't push homeowners toward a decision. Guide them toward *understanding*, and let the decision emerge naturally from the numbers.

---

**2.1b – Trust Barriers with Contractors and Sales Reps**

Homeowners have almost certainly been let down by a contractor before. A roofer who disappeared after deposit. A HVAC company that overcharged. A home warranty that covered nothing.

This history lives in the background of every conversation you have. The homeowner isn't just evaluating solar—they're evaluating *you* as a person and whether you'll be different from the people who disappointed them before.

**Signs a homeowner is guarded due to past contractor experiences:**
- Arms crossed, short answers
- "I've heard this before"
- Lots of questions about guarantees and contracts
- Reluctance to share their utility bill

**What this means for you:** Slow down. Don't push for information or commitment before you've earned it. Every genuine moment of rapport is a deposit in the trust account.

---

**2.1c – Skepticism Toward Solar Companies Specifically**

The solar industry has a reputation problem in some markets. Aggressive door-to-door tactics, misleading savings promises, and companies that went bankrupt mid-install have made some homeowners deeply skeptical of *any* solar sales rep—regardless of how they present themselves.

**Common beliefs you'll encounter:**
- "Solar companies are all the same"
- "They just want to take my money"
- "My neighbor went solar and it didn't work out"
- "I've been burned before on something like this"

**What this means for you:** Acknowledge the industry's reputation proactively. Don't pretend the skepticism doesn't exist. Reps who name the elephant in the room build trust faster than those who ignore it.

*Script example:* "I know solar reps have a certain reputation—I get it. That's actually why I lead with your numbers before I say anything about our company. If the math doesn't work for your home, I'll tell you, and we're done. Fair?"

---

**2.1d – Loss Aversion: Fear of Making a Bad Decision**

Nobel Prize-winning psychologist Daniel Kahneman's research shows that people feel the pain of a loss approximately twice as intensely as the pleasure of an equivalent gain. This is *loss aversion*, and it governs most major purchase decisions.

What this means in practice:
- A homeowner who could save $200/month on solar is not motivated by "gaining $200/month"—they're motivated by not losing $200/month to a utility company that doesn't deserve it
- The fear of "making a mistake" or "getting ripped off" is more powerful than the excitement of savings
- Homeowners will invent reasons to delay if they feel even slightly uncertain

**What this means for you:** Frame solar as *preventing a loss*, not *gaining a benefit*. "You're going to pay the utility $100,000 over the next 25 years regardless. The question is whether you pay it to them or lock it in at a lower fixed rate."

Also: Never pressure. Pressure activates loss aversion against *you*.

---

**2.1e – Decision Fatigue**

The average adult makes thousands of decisions per day. By the time you knock on a door at 5pm, that homeowner may already be mentally exhausted. Decision fatigue makes people more likely to default to "no"—not because they don't want solar, but because saying no requires less cognitive effort than evaluating your proposal.

**Signs of decision fatigue:**
- "Can you just leave me some info?"
- "Not tonight, come back another time"
- "I need to think about it"
- Glazed-over expression, short attention span

**What this means for you:**
- Keep your door pitch short (60 seconds max)
- Don't ask for a big decision at the door—ask for a small one (an appointment)
- Make every next step *easy* and *low-risk*

---

**Key Takeaways: Homeowner Psychology**

| Psychological Factor | What Rep Should Do |
|---|---|
| Status quo bias | Don't fight the current situation—show what staying costs |
| Contractor distrust | Slow down, build rapport before asking for anything |
| Solar industry skepticism | Name it, own it, differentiate immediately |
| Loss aversion | Frame savings as preventing loss, not gaining benefit |
| Decision fatigue | Keep initial ask small; save the big decision for the appointment |

**Activity:** Small group discussion — "What resistance have you personally encountered when someone tried to sell you something? What helped or hurt?" (Makes it experiential, not abstract)

**Deliverable:** Written "homeowner empathy map" — rep describes a distrustful homeowner they'll likely meet and explains their probable psychology

#### ⭐ Module 2.6 – Micro Objection Handling: Getting Past the Brush-Off (30 min)

> **NEW EXPANDED MODULE — Early objection handling is the #1 skill that separates reps who get appointments from those who get dismissed.**

Most objections at the door are *not* real objections. They are automatic, reflexive responses designed to end the interaction as quickly as possible. The homeowner isn't thinking deeply about solar — they're just trying to get back to their life.

**The Golden Rule of Micro Objections:** Don't argue. Don't defend. Ask one short, curious question and invite them back into the conversation.

---

**The 7 Most Common Door Brush-Offs + Responses:**

**1. "Not interested."**

This is the most common reflexive response. Respond with curiosity, not defense.

*"Totally fair. Quick question—have you already looked into solar, or just haven't gotten around to it yet?"*

*(If "haven't looked"):* "That's actually why I'm here—most people in this area don't realize they qualify. If your bill's over $100/month, there's probably something worth seeing. What's yours running these days?"

*(If "already looked"):* "Nice—so you know the concept makes sense. What stopped you from moving forward?" → This reopens the conversation.

---

**2. "Already have solar."**

*"Oh nice! Who'd you go with? …How's it working out? …Cool. Well, I also do battery storage and upgrades—any issues with [their system/company]?"*

Or simply: "That's awesome. Do you know anyone on this street who doesn't? Referrals actually get a cash bonus—I'm happy to leave you my card."

---

**3. "Just leave me some information."**

This is polite dismissal. Information alone almost never converts.

*"I totally can—but honestly, the info won't mean much without your specific numbers. What I'd really like to do is pull your actual roof and utility data before we meet, so I can show you something real. When are you typically home—evenings or weekends?"*

If they insist: "No problem." Leave a door hanger with a QR code + handwritten note. Circle back in 3 days.

---

**4. "I'm busy right now."**

*"I figured—that's why I'm not going to pitch you right now. I'm just booking free energy consultations in this area. Two minutes now to pick a time, and I'll do all the prep work before I come back. Thursday around 6 or Saturday morning—which is better?"*

---

**5. "I need to talk to my spouse."**

Validate this immediately—it's actually a green flag (they're interested enough to think about it).

*"Of course—that's a smart move. Is your spouse home right now? ...No? What time are you both usually home? I don't even like to show numbers until both of you are there—it just doesn't make sense any other way."*

This repositions you as someone who respects their process, not pressures it.

---

**6. "We're renting."**

*"Ah, totally makes sense—solar's really for homeowners. Do you know if your landlord owns the property? Some landlords actually do it to increase property value. Also, do you know anyone in the neighborhood who owns their home? I'm happy to give a referral bonus if they go solar."*

---

**7. "We already tried solar and it didn't work out."**

This requires empathy first.

*"Oh wow—I'm sorry to hear that. What happened, if you don't mind me asking?"*

Listen fully. Then: "That's actually one of the things I try hard to do differently—[address their specific concern]. Would you be open to a 15-minute conversation just to see if things have changed? No pressure, and if it's not right, I'll tell you."

---

**Micro Objection Handling Principles:**

1. **Never defend — get curious.** Arguing activates resistance. Questions open conversations.
2. **Validate before redirecting.** "Totally fair" / "That makes sense" / "I hear you" — then pivot.
3. **Always close on a small next step.** An appointment, a callback time, or permission to leave a card — never leave empty-handed.
4. **Match their energy.** Rushed homeowner? Be quick. Relaxed homeowner? Slow down.

**Activity:** Rapid-fire objection drill — manager throws random brush-offs, rep responds in real-time (30 seconds each, 15 rounds)

**Deliverable:** Rep writes their own 1-sentence responses to all 7 micro objections above

#### Module 3.2 – Personality Profiling (BOLT Framework) (25 min)

**Objectives:**
- Identify personality type in 2 minutes

**BOLT Overview:**
- **B** = Bull (dominant, direct, fast-paced)
- **O** = Owl (analytical, detail-oriented, slow)
- **L** = Lion (enthusiastic, social, emotional)
- **T** = Turtle (steady, risk-averse, loyal)

**How to Identify Each Type (Fast Clues):**

| Type | Clues | Communication Style |
|------|-------|---------------------|
| **Bull** | Rushed, impatient, "just give me the bottom line" | Get to the point fast, show ROI, avoid fluff |
| **Owl** | Asks detailed questions, wants to see data, skeptical | Provide charts, data, warranties, technical specs |
| **Lion** | Friendly, talkative, shares personal stories | Build rapport, ask about family, make it fun |
| **Turtle** | Hesitant, risk-averse, "need to think about it" | Emphasize safety, social proof, no-pressure approach |

**Adaptation Strategies:**
- **Bull:** "Here's the deal—your bill's $200/month. Solar locks it at $150. That's $600/year saved, $15K over 25 years. Want to move forward?"
- **Owl:** "Let me show you the degradation curve, warranty terms, and production guarantee. Here's the engineering report..."
- **Lion:** "Your neighbors are going to be so impressed. Plus, you're doing something great for your family—how does that feel?"
- **Turtle:** "No pressure today. Let's just see if the numbers make sense. Over 1,200 families in this area already did it—you're in good company."

**Mixed Couples (Common Scenario):**
- Husband = Bull, Wife = Turtle → Show ROI fast (Bull), then emphasize safety/social proof (Turtle)

#### ⭐ Module 3.3 – Real-World Homeowner Scenario Library (45 min)

> **NEW MODULE — Prepare reps for the 6 most challenging homeowner types they'll encounter in the field.**

Most training prepares reps for willing homeowners. Real life is different. This module trains reps to recognize difficult homeowner archetypes, understand what's driving their behavior, and adapt accordingly.

---

**Scenario 1: The Burned Homeowner**
*"I had a contractor completely screw me over last year."*

**What's happening psychologically:**
This homeowner's trust has been violated. They're carrying the wound of a past bad experience and are subconsciously looking for evidence that you're the same. Every word you say will be filtered through a "can I trust this person?" lens. They need you to prove you're different — but they'll be watching closely for any sign that you're not.

**What the rep should focus on:**
- Don't defend the industry or minimize their experience
- Validate fully: "That's awful — and honestly, some companies in this space have earned that reputation."
- Ask what specifically went wrong (shows you care and you're gathering intel)
- Move slower than usual. Earn each piece of trust before asking for more
- Offer social proof: reviews, local references, license numbers, warranties in writing
- Frame your company's process differently: "Here's what we do that most companies don't..."
- Never rush to close. Let them set the pace.

> 🤖 **AI Simulation Link**
> - **Scenario:** "Burned Beth" — Homeowner who shares that a previous solar company took a deposit and never showed up. She's skeptical but curious.
> - **Skill Being Trained:** Empathy first, trust rebuilding, slowing the conversation down, transparent differentiation

---

**Scenario 2: The Analytical Homeowner Who Thinks They Already Know Solar**
*"I've done a lot of research. I know how this works."*

**What's happening psychologically:**
This homeowner has invested time and ego into their solar research. They feel intellectually prepared, and they're subtly testing you to see if you know more than they do — or if you'll try to oversimplify or mislead them. If you talk down to them, you'll lose them. If you try to bluff through technical questions, they'll catch you. If you respect their intelligence and go deeper than they expected, you'll earn enormous credibility.

**What the rep should focus on:**
- Acknowledge their research: "Sounds like you've done your homework—that actually makes this easier."
- Don't repeat basic concepts they clearly know. Ask what they've learned and where their questions are.
- Use specific technical language (NEM, degradation curves, DC/AC ratio, dealer fees)
- Be honest when you don't know something: "That's a great question — let me get you the exact numbers from our engineer."
- Ask: "Based on your research, what's the one thing that's been holding you back?" (Often reveals the real objection)
- Position yourself as a resource, not a salesperson

> 🤖 **AI Simulation Link**
> - **Scenario:** "Analytical Alan" — Homeowner who immediately asks about NEM 3.0 impacts and dealer fees in loan products. He wants specifics, not slides.
> - **Skill Being Trained:** Technical confidence, credibility under scrutiny, redirecting to the real decision

---

**Scenario 3: The Busy Homeowner**
*"I've only got a few minutes."*

**What's happening psychologically:**
This homeowner is time-constrained (or uses busyness as a shield). They're not necessarily uninterested — they just have a high threshold for deciding to invest their attention. They need to feel immediately that this conversation is worth their time. If you start slow or build too much preamble, you'll lose them before you get to the value.

**What the rep should focus on:**
- Accept the time constraint without argument: "I respect that — I'll be quick."
- Lead immediately with the most compelling hook for their situation: "Your bill's over [X], right? Fifteen minutes from now, I can show you whether you're leaving money on the table."
- Skip rapport-building niceties and get to curiosity questions fast
- If they can't sit now, set a specific appointment time: "What if I came back Thursday at 6 — would you have 20 minutes then?"
- Match their energy (fast, direct, no fluff)

> 🤖 **AI Simulation Link**
> - **Scenario:** "Busy Bob" — Homeowner answers the door in work clothes, glancing at watch, says "I've got ten minutes max."
> - **Skill Being Trained:** Pacing and brevity, high-value hook, fast appointment set when time runs out

---

**Scenario 4: The Friendly Homeowner Who Won't Commit**
*Great conversation... but never a close.*

**What's happening psychologically:**
This homeowner genuinely likes you and enjoys the interaction. But likability is not the same as decision readiness. They're often conflict-avoidant — they don't want to disappoint you by saying no, so they keep the conversation pleasant and non-committal. They'll agree with everything you say and then say "let me think about it" at the end.

**What the rep should focus on:**
- Enjoy the rapport, but recognize it's a trap if you don't steer toward commitment
- Use micro-commits throughout: "Make sense so far?" / "If the numbers worked, would anything stop you from moving forward?"
- When they say "I'll think about it," respond with specific curiosity: "Of course. What specifically do you want to think through? Let's work through it together right now."
- Identify if there's a hidden blocker (spouse, finances, fear of commitment) and address it directly
- Use assumptive language: "Let's get you scheduled" not "Would you want to schedule?"

> 🤖 **AI Simulation Link**
> - **Scenario:** "Friendly Frank" — Homeowner laughs at your jokes, tells you about his garden, says "great presentation" and then "I'll definitely think about it."
> - **Skill Being Trained:** Steering likable conversations toward commitment, smoke-out objections, assumptive close

**Scenario 5: The Homeowner Who Already Has Solar**
*"We went solar three years ago."*

**What's happening psychologically:**
This homeowner sees themselves as already solved. But they may have genuine upgrade opportunities, battery storage options, or they may be on an older NEM agreement that you can help them understand. They may also have had a bad experience. And they're almost always willing to give referrals if you approach them right.

**What the rep should focus on:**
- Lead with genuine curiosity, not sales: "That's great — how's it working out?"
- Ask about their system: who installed, what size, are they happy with the production, do they know about storage options
- If there's dissatisfaction, position an upgrade or second opinion
- If they're happy, pivot to referrals: "I love that — would you be open to a referral bonus if any of your neighbors go solar through us?"
- Collect their info as a future battery storage or upgrade lead

> 🤖 **AI Simulation Link**
> - **Scenario:** "Solar Sam" — Homeowner who already has solar but is annoyed by how his monitoring app works and doesn't fully understand his true-up bill.

**Scenario 6: The Homeowner Who Trusts Their Utility Company**
*"I'm fine with [Utility]. They've always treated me well."*

**What's happening psychologically:**
This homeowner has a relationship — or at least a comfort level — with a familiar institution. They don't see the utility as an adversary. They may not know about rate increase history, or they may feel that loyalty to a known brand is safer than switching to something new. They're not hostile to you — they just don't see a problem worth solving.

**What the rep should focus on:**
- Don't attack the utility. Respect their comfort with familiarity.
- Educate gently: "That's great to hear. Quick question — do you know how much [Utility] has raised rates over the last 10 years?"
- Show the historical rate increase data as a factual conversation, not an accusation
- Reframe solar: "You'd still be with the utility — you'd just be using less of their power, at a rate you control."
- Make it a hedge, not a replacement: "This isn't about leaving [Utility]. It's about not being 100% dependent on their pricing decisions."

#### Module 4.1 – Utility Bill Deep Dive: Advanced Analysis (25 min)

**Objectives:**
- Perform surgical bill analysis in front of homeowner
- Build credibility through technical mastery
- Identify hidden savings opportunities

**The Bill Autopsy Process (Live Demo):**
- Pull up bill with homeowner watching
- Narrate your analysis (builds trust)
- "Let me show you exactly where your money's going..."

**Step-by-Step Breakdown:**

**1. Annual Usage Calculation**
- Locate 12-month usage history
- Add up total kWh (e.g., 12,000 kWh/year)
- Calculate daily average: 12,000 ÷ 365 = 33 kWh/day
- "So you're using about 33 kilowatt-hours per day. That's actually pretty typical for a [home size] in [area]."

**2. Rate Structure Identification**

| Rate Type | How to Identify | Impact on Solar |
|-----------|-----------------|-----------------|
| **Flat Rate** | Same ¢/kWh all month | Simple offset calculation |
| **Tiered** | Rate increases with usage | Solar saves MORE (eliminates high tiers) |
| **Time-of-Use (TOU)** | Different rates by time of day | Requires strategic usage or battery |
| **Demand Charges** | Peak usage penalties (commercial) | Solar may not eliminate fully |

**3. Seasonal Spike Analysis**
- "I see your summer bills hit $280 in July. What's running that high?"
- (Usually AC → validate system sizing)
- "Winter drops to $110. That's actually good—means solar will overproduce in winter, bank credits for summer."

**4. Inflation Projection (Compliant Version)**
- Pull up utility's historical rate increases (public record)
- "Your utility has increased rates an average of 5.8% per year over the last decade."
- Show 10-year projection:
  - Current bill: $180/month = $2,160/year
  - Year 5 (at 5.8%): $2,850/year
  - Year 10: $3,760/year
  - **Total paid to utility over 10 years: ~$29,400**
- "Solar locks your rate. So instead of this climbing, it stays flat."

**5. True-Up Logic (For NEM Customers)**
- "You'll get monthly credits when you overproduce."
- "Once a year, the utility 'trues up'—settles the account."
- "If you overproduced, they pay you wholesale (~3¢/kWh). If you underproduced, you pay them retail."
- "That's why we size systems to 100–110% of your usage—cushion for rate changes."

**6. Offset Percentage Explanation**
- "100% offset means the system produces exactly what you use annually."
- "We typically recommend 105–110% to account for degradation and usage increases."
- "You'll still have a small bill—connection fees, taxes—usually $10–20/month."

**Compliance Warning:**
- ❌ NEVER say: "Your bill will be $0" or "Guaranteed to eliminate your bill"
- ✅ ALWAYS say: "We're offsetting [X]% of your usage. You'll still have connection fees."

**OPTION 1: SOLAR LOAN (Most Common)**

**How It Works:**

- Owns the system, gets all incentives

**Pros:**
- ✅ Homeowner gets 30% federal tax credit (~$9,000 on $30K system)
- ✅ Builds home equity
- ✅ Can pay off early without penalty (usually)
- ✅ Best long-term savings

**Cons:**
- ❌ Requires decent credit (typically 650+)
- ❌ Monthly payment (though often lower than utility bill)
- ❌ Homeowner responsible for maintenance (though minimal)

**Payment Calculation Example:**
- System cost: $30,000
- Federal tax credit: -$9,000 (30%)
- Net cost: $21,000
- 20-year loan at 5.99% APR
- **Monthly payment: ~$150**
- Current utility bill: $180/month
- **Net savings: $30/month from day one**

**The 18-Month Strategy:**
- Some loans offer 18 months same-as-cash
- Homeowner applies tax credit (~$9,000) as principal payment in Year 2
- Drops payment significantly
- Script: "You'll have a higher payment for 18 months, then you apply your tax credit and it drops."

**Dealer Fee Reality:**
- Many loans include dealer fees (built into rate)
- Always disclose: "This rate includes financing costs. You can pay cash and avoid them."

---

**OPTION 2: SOLAR LEASE**

**How It Works:**
- Solar company owns the system
- Homeowner pays fixed monthly lease payment
- Company handles maintenance

**Pros:**
- ✅ $0 down (usually)
- ✅ No maintenance responsibility
- ✅ Predictable payment

**Cons:**
- ❌ Solar company gets the tax credit
- ❌ Escalator clause (payment increases 2–3% annually)
- ❌ Complicates home sale

**When to Recommend:** Low credit score, no tax liability, budget-conscious, shorter-term homeowner (5–7 years)

---

**OPTION 3: POWER PURCHASE AGREEMENT (PPA)**

**How It Works:**
- Solar company owns system
- Homeowner pays per kWh produced (e.g., 12¢/kWh)
- Company handles maintenance

**Pros:**
- ✅ Only pay for power produced
- ✅ $0 down
- ✅ No maintenance responsibility

**Cons:**
- ❌ Rate escalates annually
- ❌ Complicates home sale
- ❌ No tax credit for homeowner

**When to Recommend:** States with high utility rates, risk-averse personalities, homeowners who distrust fixed payments

---

**Financing Comparison Table:**

| Feature | Loan | Lease | PPA |
|---------|------|-------|-----|
| **Ownership** | Homeowner | Company | Company |
| **Tax Credit** | Homeowner gets it | Company keeps it | Company keeps it |
| **Upfront Cost** | $0–20% down | $0 | $0 |
| **Monthly Payment** | Fixed | Fixed (with escalator) | Variable (per kWh) |
| **Total Savings** | Highest | Medium | Medium |
| **Best For** | Long-term owners, good credit | Low credit, simplicity | Risk-averse |

**Credit Score Thresholds:**

| Score | Options | What to Expect |
|-------|---------|----------------|
| 720+ | All options, best rates | 2.99–4.99% APR loans |
| 680–719 | Most loans, lease | 5.99–7.99% APR |
| 640–679 | Some loans, lease, PPA | 8.99–11.99% APR or lease only |
| Below 640 | Lease/PPA only | May require co-signer or cash |

**How to Handle "What's the Interest Rate?" Objection:**

*"Great question. Yes, it's 7.99%, which includes financing costs. But here's what matters: you're currently paying a 100% interest rate to the utility—you pay forever and own nothing. With solar, you're financing an asset that eliminates that payment. Think of it like a mortgage—the rate matters less than the fact you're building equity instead of renting."*

#### Module 4.4 – Net Metering & Battery Storage (25 min)

**Net Metering Logic:**
- "Think of the grid as your battery."
- When you overproduce → credits go to your account
- At night → draw from grid, use credits
- Annual true-up: over-produced = utility pays you (wholesale); under-produced = you pay them (retail)

**NEM 2.0 vs NEM 3.0 (adapt for your state):**
- NEM 2.0: Full retail credit for excess production
- NEM 3.0: Reduced credits (~75% of retail), incentivizes battery storage

**Battery Storage (Realistic Expectations):**

| Batteries DO | Batteries DON'T DO |
|---|---|
| Store excess solar for nighttime | Make you truly off-grid |
| Provide backup power during outages | Power your entire home indefinitely |
| Reduce grid reliance | Eliminate connection fees |

**When Batteries Make Sense:** Frequent outages, TOU rates, NEM 3.0 states, energy independence priority

**When to Skip the Battery:** Reliable grid + NEM 2.0, budget-conscious, homeowner rarely loses power

#### Module 5.1 – Objection Handling Psychology (20 min)

**Objectives:**
- Reframe objections as requests for more information
- Respond calmly, not defensively
- Turn objections into closes

**Core Reframe:**
An objection is not a "no." It is a *request for reassurance* or a *sign of engagement*. If someone wasn't interested at all, they wouldn't object — they'd just leave.

**The A.C.A. Objection Framework:**
1. **Acknowledge** — Validate the concern without agreeing with it
2. **Clarify** — Ask a question to understand the *real* concern beneath the stated one
3. **Answer** — Provide the specific information they actually need

**What NOT to Do:**
- ❌ Get defensive ("Well actually, that's not true...")
- ❌ Immediately throw more information at them
- ❌ Ignore the objection and keep presenting
- ❌ Cave immediately ("You're right, I understand, no problem")

---

#### Module 5.2 – The Top 10 Closing Objections + Responses (45 min)

**1. "I need to think about it."**

*Clarify:* "Of course—that's a smart move. What specifically do you want to think through? I want to make sure you have everything you need."

*(If vague):* "When people say that, it usually means one of three things: the timing isn't right, the money doesn't feel comfortable, or something doesn't fully make sense yet. Which one is closest for you?"

---

**2. "It's too expensive."**

*Clarify:* "When you say expensive—is it the monthly payment or the total system cost that feels off?"

*(If monthly):* Show payment vs. current utility bill side by side. "You're already paying this. Solar replaces that payment with one that's lower and fixed."

*(If total):* "I hear you. Here's the thing: the $30K system isn't an expense—it's a 25-year investment that pays for itself. The question isn't 'can I afford solar?' It's 'can I afford to keep paying the utility?'"

---

**3. "I want to get other quotes."**

*"Smart. You should. Here's what I'd ask when you talk to others: [specific differentiating questions about warranties, installer experience, monitoring, cancellation policy]. Most reps won't answer those clearly—that's how you'll know."*

Then: "While you're doing that, do you want me to put a preliminary design together so you have something to compare against? No obligation."

---

**4. "We're not ready right now."**

*"Totally fair. Can I ask—what would need to change for the timing to be right?"*

If answer is "nothing specific" → Use urgency (tax credit timeline, utility rate increases)
If answer is "specific event" → Set a follow-up for after that event, get it in CRM immediately

---

**5. "I need to talk to my spouse."**

Prevention is better than cure (Module 3.6). But if you're here:

*"Of course—I completely understand. I actually prefer not to move forward without both of you on board. Can we get a time when you're both available? I'd rather do this once, right, with everyone there."*

---

**6. "I don't trust solar companies."**

*"That's a legitimate concern—some have earned that reputation. Can I ask what specifically you've heard? [...] Here's what I can show you: [licenses, reviews, BBB rating, local references, warranty terms in writing]. Would that help?"*

---

**7. "What if I sell my house?"**

*"Great question. A few options: (1) The loan can sometimes transfer to the buyer as part of the home sale. (2) You can pay off the loan from the sale proceeds—and studies show solar adds more to the sale price than the remaining balance. (3) Some buyers see solar as a major benefit and will pay more for it. Most of our clients who've sold actually came out ahead."*

---

**8. "What about the roof warranty?"**

*"Good catch. Our installer carries a 10-year workmanship warranty. If anything goes wrong with the roof due to installation, we handle it. And if you have an existing roof warranty, we can review it with your roofer before signing—we do this all the time."*

---

**9. "Solar technology will be better in a few years."**

*"It will—and a year from now it'll be even better. The technology has been improving for 40 years. But here's the thing: the system you install today is already excellent, and every month you wait is a month you're paying the utility. The savings you delay are gone forever. That said—what specifically are you hoping improves?"*

---

**10. "I can't afford the payment right now."**

*Clarify:* "What's comfortable for you monthly?"

If there's a gap: Explore longer loan terms, lease options, or delayed start dates. If truly unqualified: Disqualify gracefully. "I hear you. Let's talk in [X months] when [condition improves]. I'll set a reminder."

#### Module 5.3 – Closing Techniques (30 min)

**Objectives:**

**The Core Principle: Always Be Transitioning**

Closing is not a single moment — it's a series of micro-commitments that naturally progress to the signature. If you've done discovery and presentation right, the close should feel like a logical next step, not a high-pressure moment.

**Closing Technique 1: The Assumptive Close**
- "Alright, let's get your application started."
- "So what we'll do next is schedule your site survey—what day works best?"
- *(Assumes the decision has been made; proceeds to logistics)*

**Closing Technique 2: The Summary Close**
- "So we've looked at your bill, designed the system, run the numbers, and the math works. You're saving $30/month from day one, and $48,000 over 25 years. Based on all that—does it make sense to move forward?"

**Closing Technique 3: The Decision Matrix**
- Draw a T-chart: "Go Solar" vs. "Wait"
- Walk through benefits of going now vs. costs of waiting
- Let them see it visually — often the decision becomes obvious

**Closing Technique 4: The Silence Close**
- Ask for the sale once, clearly
- Then stop talking
- The first person to speak loses; make it them

**Closing Technique 5: The Last Objection**
- "Is there anything else holding you back, or is this something you're ready to move forward with?"
- *(Forces them to surface the real final objection or commit)*

---

**Activity 7.1: Pre-Field Briefing (30 min)**
*Location: Office or coffee shop*

**Objectives:**
- Review Day 7 agenda
- Set expectations for shadowing
- Address any last-minute questions

**Manager Script:**
*"Today you're going to watch me run 3 appointments. Your job is to observe, take notes, and learn. You'll see how I handle objections in real-time, how I control the room, and how I close. After lunch, you'll run an appointment and I'll observe. Then we'll debrief. Sound good?"*

**What to Watch For (Give Rep This Checklist):**
- ✅ How manager positions themselves (seating, body language)
- ✅ How manager handles interruptions (kids, phone calls, TV)
- ✅ How manager adapts to personality types
- ✅ How manager handles objections (tonality, phrasing)
- ✅ How manager asks for the sale (assumptive close, silence close, etc.)
- ✅ **NEW:** Which homeowner scenario type is this? (refer to Module 3.3)
- ✅ **NEW:** Which step of the Solar Conversation Framework are we in?

**Deliverable:** Observation checklist (rep fills out after each appointment)

---

**Activity 7.2: Appointment #1 — Shadow & Observe**
*Duration: 60–90 min (includes drive time)*

**Rep's Role:**
- Sit quietly, take notes
- Do NOT interrupt (unless manager invites you to speak)
- Observe everything (seating, tonality, objection handling, close)

**Post-Appointment Debrief (10 min in car):**
- Manager: "What did you notice?"
- Rep shares observations
- Manager: "Here's what I was thinking when [X happened]..."
- Rep asks clarifying questions

---

**Activity 7.3: Appointment #2 — Shadow & Observe**
*Duration: 60–90 min*

**Post-Appointment Debrief:**
- Manager: "What would you have done differently?"
- Rep critiques constructively
- Manager explains alternative approaches

---

**Activity 7.4: Appointment #3 — Shadow & Observe**
*Duration: 60–90 min*

**Post-Appointment Debrief:**
- Manager: "You're up next after lunch. Based on what you've seen, what's your game plan?"
- Rep outlines their approach
- Manager coaches adjustments

---

### Lunch Break (30–60 min)

**Objectives:**
- Decompress and process learnings
- Build confidence for afternoon appointment
- Review notes and scripts

**Manager's Job:**
- Encourage rep ("You've got this. You're ready.")
- Answer any last-minute questions
- Confirm afternoon appointment details

### Afternoon Session (3–4 hours): Rep Runs Appointment (Manager Observes)

---

**Activity 7.5: Pre-Appointment Prep (15 min)**

**Manager Asks:**
- "What do you know about this homeowner?" (review lead info)
- "What's your opener?" (rep rehearses)
- "What objections do you expect?" (anticipate challenges)
- "Which homeowner type do you think this might be?" (scenario awareness)
- "How will you close?" (assumptive, decision matrix, etc.)

**Manager Coaches:**
- "Remember to control the seating."
- "If they object, pause before responding."
- "Don't forget to ask for referrals after you close."

---

**Activity 7.6: Rep Runs Full Appointment (Manager Observes)**
*Duration: 60–90 min*

**Manager's Role:**
- Sit quietly in the corner (do NOT interrupt unless rep asks for help)
- Take notes on performance (strengths + areas for improvement)
- Only step in if rep completely freezes or homeowner asks manager a question

**Rep's Job:**
- Run full appointment (discovery → presentation → objection handling → close)
- Treat this as real (not practice)
- Ask for manager's help if stuck (but try to handle it first)

---

**Activity 7.7: Post-Appointment Debrief (20 min in car)**

**Manager Feedback Framework:**

**1. What Went Well (Start Positive):**
- "You did a great job with [specific example]."
- "Your tonality was perfect when [specific moment]."
- "I loved how you handled [objection]."

**2. What to Improve (Constructive):**
- "One thing I'd suggest: [specific adjustment]."
- "Next time, try [alternative approach]."
- "You missed an opportunity to [specific action]."

**3. Overall Assessment:**
- ✅ **Field-Ready:** "You're good to go. Start setting appointments and close deals."
- ⚠️ **Almost There:** "One more shadowing day, then you're ready."
- ❌ **Needs More Training:** "Let's do another week of role-play and shadowing."

---

**Activity 7.8: Certification Sign-Off (If Passed)**

**Rep Receives:**

- Business cards, yard signs, marketing materials
